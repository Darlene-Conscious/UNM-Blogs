What the Gender Pay Gap Actually Measures

(And Why Everyone Gets It Wrong)

"But it's illegal to pay women less for the same work! The gender pay gap is a myth!"

If you've ever mentioned the gender pay gap in conversation, you've probably heard this objection. And here's the thing: they're not entirely wrong. It IS illegal in Australia to pay someone less because of their gender for doing identical work. The Fair Work Act 2009 explicitly prohibits it.

But that's precisely why so many people fundamentally misunderstand what the gender pay gap actually measures — and why that misunderstanding is so dangerous.

What People THINK the Gender Pay Gap Means

Most people believe the gender pay gap means this: a man and a woman sit side-by-side in identical roles, doing identical work, with identical qualifications and experience — and the woman gets paid less simply because she's a woman.

If that were all the gender pay gap measured, critics would be right to call it a myth. Direct wage discrimination for identical work, while it still happens, has been illegal in Australia since the Equal Pay Case of 1972.

What People Think It Measures:

Marketing Manager (Male): $120,000/year
Marketing Manager (Female): $95,000/year
Same company, same role, same experience — different pay because of gender

But that's not what the gender pay gap measures. Not even close.

What the Gender Pay Gap ACTUALLY Measures

The gender pay gap is the difference between what ALL women earn across the entire Australian economy compared to what ALL men earn. It doesn't compare individual job titles. It doesn't account for hours worked, industry, or seniority. It compares the total remuneration of women as a group to men as a group.

As of 2024, Australia's national gender pay gap sits at 11.5% for base salary. This means for every dollar men earn on average, women earn 88 cents. That's $242.30 less per week, or $12,600 less per year.

When we look at total remuneration (which includes bonuses, overtime, and additional payments), the gap widens dramatically. The Workplace Gender Equality Agency reports a total remuneration gender pay gap of 21.7%. For women in male-dominated industries like construction, that gap reaches 31.8%.

❌ THE MYTH

"Women and men doing the same job get paid differently."

✓ THE REALITY

"Women and men don't end up in the same jobs — and that's exactly the problem."

So If It's Not About Direct Discrimination, What IS It About?

The gender pay gap exists because of three interconnected structural realities:

1. Women Are Concentrated in Lower-Paying Industries

Women dominate industries like childcare, aged care, nursing, teaching, and social work. Men dominate industries like mining, construction, engineering, and finance. The "women's industries" pay significantly less — not because they require less skill, education, or physical labour, but because they're women's industries.

Real-World Example: Childcare vs. Parking Enforcement

Childcare Worker (95% female)
Average salary: $65,000-$70,000/year
Requirements: Certificate III or Diploma in Early Childhood Education

Parking Inspector (predominantly male)
Average salary: $67,000-$73,000/year
Requirements: High school education, brief training

Both jobs require qualifications and training. One involves the safety and development of children's brains. The other issues parking tickets. Guess which one pays more?

2. "Women's Work" Gets Devalued BECAUSE Women Do It

Here's the part that really undermines the "women just choose lower-paying fields" argument: fields don't start out low-paying. They BECOME low-paying when women enter them in significant numbers.

Take computer programming. In the 1940s through the 1960s, programming was considered clerical work — tedious, detail-oriented, low-status labour. It was "women's work." Women like the ENIAC programmers (Kay McNulty, Betty Jennings, Betty Snyder, Marlyn Wescoff, Fran Bilas, and Ruth Lichterman) pioneered the field. Programming was dismissed as requiring "no thinking and no skill."

But when the importance and prestige of computing became apparent in the 1970s and 1980s, the industry underwent deliberate "masculinization." Professional standards were created. University programs emerged that favoured men. Aptitude tests prioritised stereotypically masculine traits. And suddenly, programming wasn't clerical work anymore — it was prestigious, high-status, high-paying work.

By 1969, women in computing earned a median of $7,763 while men earned $11,193 — for the same work. By the 1990s, women had been almost entirely pushed out. Today, programming is 80% male and pays six figures at entry level.

The work didn't change. The gender composition changed. And the pay followed.

This pattern repeats across industries. When a field becomes female-dominated, wages stagnate or decline. Teaching, nursing, social work — all followed the same trajectory.

3. The Language of Job Titles Hides the Pay Gap

Here's where it gets insidious: even when women and men do virtually identical work, the language used to describe their roles makes women's work seem less valuable — and that language difference justifies lower pay.

Job titles and descriptions use gendered language that subtly signals who the role is "for" and how much it's worth. Masculine-coded words like "competitive," "dominant," "decisive," and "ambitious" signal high-status, high-paying roles. Feminine-coded words like "supportive," "collaborative," "nurturing," and "committed" signal lower-status, lower-paying work.

But here's the crucial part: roles with similar responsibilities get described differently based on gender composition, which then justifies different pay scales.

Real-World Example: HR Manager vs. Operations Manager

HR Manager (73% female)
Average salary: $100,000-$111,000/year
Described as: "supportive," "collaborative," "interpersonal," "committed," "connect"
Framed as: Assisting, coordinating, supporting business operations

Operations Manager (predominantly male)
Average salary: $115,000-$132,000/year
Described as: "driven," "decisive," "competitive," "autonomous," "lead"
Framed as: Directing, controlling, driving business strategy

Both roles manage people, processes, and strategic business functions. Both require similar qualifications. But the Operations Manager earns 15-20% more because the language frames it as leadership rather than support.

This isn't accidental. Research shows that when job descriptions contain masculine-coded language like "assertive," "analytical," and "strong," they signal higher pay and status. When they contain feminine-coded language like "helpful," "flexible," and "patient," they signal lower value.

A 2017 study of 77,000 UK job advertisements found that "lead" was the most frequently used masculine word in job descriptions. "Support" was the most used feminine word. One signals authority and command. The other signals assistance and service. Both might describe the exact same work — but they don't command the same pay.

How Gendered Language Shapes Pay:

Same Work, Different Framing:

Masculine: "Drive strategic initiatives" → Higher pay
Feminine: "Support strategic initiatives" → Lower pay

Masculine: "Lead cross-functional teams" → Higher pay
Feminine: "Coordinate cross-functional teams" → Lower pay

Masculine: "Execute decisive action" → Higher pay
Feminine: "Implement collaborative solutions" → Lower pay

The work is identical. The outcomes are identical. But the language codes one as more valuable, more powerful, more worthy of compensation. And because women are more likely to be in roles with feminine-coded titles and descriptions, this language gap becomes a pay gap.

4. Women Hit Structural Barriers Men Don't Face

Even when women enter high-paying fields, they face barriers men don't:

The Motherhood Penalty: Women's careers stall or regress when they have children. They're more likely to take parental leave (which counts against them for promotion), work part-time (which pays less per hour AND limits advancement), and shoulder the majority of unpaid domestic labour (which restricts their availability for networking, overtime, or relocation).

The Fatherhood Bonus: Meanwhile, men's earnings actually INCREASE after becoming fathers. Employers perceive them as more committed, more stable, more deserving of advancement.

The Negotiation Backlash: Research consistently shows that when women negotiate for higher pay, they face social penalties men don't experience. They're seen as "difficult," "aggressive," or "not a team player." Men who negotiate the same way are seen as confident leaders.

The Part-Time Penalty: Women are far more likely to work part-time (usually due to caregiving responsibilities). Part-time work in Australia pays less per hour than full-time work for the same role, offers fewer opportunities for advancement, and rarely includes bonuses or additional payments that inflate total remuneration.

Why "Women's Choices" Is a Dangerous Myth

The most common defence of the pay gap goes like this: "Women CHOOSE lower-paying fields. They CHOOSE to work part-time. They CHOOSE not to negotiate. It's not discrimination — it's personal choice."

But let's examine what "choice" actually means:

Is it a "choice" when childcare costs more than most women's wages, making it economically irrational to work full-time?

Is it a "choice" when women who negotiate face documented career penalties that men don't?

Is it a "choice" when entire industries become devalued the moment women enter them in significant numbers?

Is it a "choice" when girls are steered away from STEM fields from childhood, through subtle (and not-so-subtle) messaging about what's "appropriate" for their gender?

Is it a "choice" when workplaces are designed around the assumption that someone else (hint: a woman) is handling all the unpaid domestic labour?

"Choice" isn't choice when the entire system is designed to channel women into undervalued roles, penalise them for caregiving, and reward men for the exact same negotiation behaviours that get women labeled as "difficult."

Why This Misunderstanding Is So Dangerous

When people believe the gender pay gap is about direct discrimination for identical work, they dismiss the entire problem as a myth. After all, that kind of discrimination is already illegal!

But the gender pay gap ISN'T about employers deliberately paying women less for identical work (though that happens too). It's about a system that:

• Consistently devalues work that women do
• Channels women into lower-paid industries and roles
• Penalises women for having children while rewarding men
• Makes it economically irrational for women to work in ways that lead to high earnings
• Rewards men for negotiation behaviours that get women penalised
• Structures workplaces around the assumption of unpaid female domestic labour

And that's actually WORSE than simple discrimination. Because it's harder to see. Harder to prove. Harder to fight. Harder to fix.

The gender pay gap isn't a myth. It's a measurement of systemic inequality so embedded in our economic structures that most people can't even see it.

What This Means for You

If you're a woman in Australia, the gender pay gap affects you whether you believe in it or not. It affects your current earnings. Your superannuation. Your lifetime wealth. Your housing security at 60.

Understanding what it actually measures is the first step toward fighting it. Because once you see the system clearly, you can make strategic choices:

• Choose industries and roles with an eye toward long-term earning potential, not just passion
• Document your value obsessively, so you have evidence when you negotiate
• Negotiate anyway, despite the backlash risk, because the cost of not negotiating is higher
• Plan for the motherhood penalty by maximising earnings before children
• Build financial independence that doesn't depend on a partner
• Refuse to accept "women's work" being valued less than equivalent men's work

The Bottom Line

The gender pay gap isn't about women and men in identical roles getting different pay checks. It's about a labour market that systematically undervalues women's work, channels women into lower-paid roles, penalises them for caregiving, and rewards them less for the same qualifications and effort as men.

That's not a myth. That's structural inequality.

And the fact that so many people misunderstand it? That's precisely why it persists.

Now you know what the gender pay gap actually measures. The question is: what are you going to do about it?
