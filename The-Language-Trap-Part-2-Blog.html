What to Watch For: Your Audit Checklist

Here's how to audit your current job description and performance reviews for language that's keeping you underpaid:

üîç Language Trap Warning Signs

  • Support/Assist/Help: These words position you as secondary. Count how many times your job description uses "support" vs. "lead," "deliver," or "execute." If it's more than 3:1, you're being linguistically devalued.
  • Collaborative/Team-oriented/Nurturing: These are positive traits, but if they dominate your description while a male colleague in a similar role is described as "strategic" or "decisive," you're in the language trap.
  • Coordinator vs. Manager: If you're managing projects, budgets, and teams but your title says "Coordinator," you're being paid less for the same work. "Coordinator" is feminine-coded; "Manager" is masculine-coded.
  • Vague performance feedback: If your review says you're "doing great" but gives no concrete steps toward promotion, you're being sidelined with praise.
  • Personality-based criticism: If you've ever been told you're "too aggressive," "too emotional," "not a team player," or "abrasive"‚Äîbut male colleagues with identical behaviors are praised‚Äîthis is gendered language justifying your exclusion from advancement.
  • Communal language dominates technical language: If your job description emphasizes interpersonal skills ("builds relationships," "creates supportive environment") over technical skills ("executes strategy," "delivers results"), you're being framed as a soft-skill worker‚Äîwhich pays less.
