The Study That Changed Everything

In 2011, researchers conducted a groundbreaking study analyzing thousands of job advertisements. What they discovered was staggering: job ads for male-dominated occupations used significantly more masculine-coded language than ads for female-dominated roles.

Job descriptions featuring masculine-coded language led women to perceive those roles as having fewer women, reduced their sense of belonging, and made the positions less appealing‚Äîeven when women were objectively qualified.

Here's the critical finding: when women saw masculine-coded job descriptions, their interest in the position dropped significantly. But when men saw feminine-coded language? It barely affected them at all.

The trap only works in one direction.

What Is Gendered Language?

Gendered language refers to words that are culturally associated with masculine or feminine stereotypes. These aren't obvious gender markers like "he" or "she"‚Äîthey're subtle descriptors that signal who "belongs" in a role.

The Real-World Impact

Goldman Sachs and the Word "Aggressive"

Goldman Sachs discovered that after removing the word "aggressive" from their job postings, their applications from women increased dramatically‚Äîhelping them achieve close to a 50-50 gender balance in hiring.

One word. One change. Measurable results.

Emergency Medicine Job Ads

A 2022 study analyzing job advertisements in emergency medicine‚Äîa male-dominated field‚Äîfound that EM job ads contained significantly more masculine-coded language than feminine-coded language. This linguistic pattern mirrors hiring outcomes: women remain underrepresented in emergency medicine leadership roles.

Why does this matter? Because when job descriptions are loaded with masculine-coded language and inflated requirements, women self-select out. The language doesn't just describe the job‚Äîit gatekeeps who even tries.

Why This Matters Beyond Hiring

Gendered language doesn't stop at job ads. It's embedded in:

Performance reviews: Women receive feedback that they're "too aggressive" when assertive, but "not leadership material" when collaborative. Men receive praise for the exact same behaviors.

Salary negotiation scripts: Women are advised to be "collaborative" and emphasize "team contributions" in negotiations. Men are told to be "assertive" and "demonstrate their value." The feminine approach results in lower offers.

Industry classification: Fields become "feminine" when described with care-oriented language (teaching, nursing, social work) and "masculine" when described with technical language (engineering, construction, finance). The feminine fields pay less‚Äînot because the work is less valuable, but because the language devalues it.

What You Can Do

1. Learn to Spot the Pattern

Read job descriptions with a critical eye. Count how many times you see words like "supportive," "collaborative," "nurturing," or "interpersonal." Then look at the salary range. You'll start to see the correlation.

2. Rewrite Your Own Narrative

In your resume, cover letters, and interviews, strategically use masculine-coded language to describe your achievements:

‚ùå Instead of: "Collaborated with stakeholders to improve processes"
‚úÖ Try: "Drove process improvements across stakeholder groups"

‚ùå Instead of: "Nurtured client relationships"
‚úÖ Try: "Built and executed strategic client partnerships"

You're not lying. You're translating your work into the language that gets rewarded.

3. Negotiate Using Masculine Language

When negotiating salary, avoid feminine-coded phrases like "I hope we can find something that works for everyone" or "I'd really appreciate if we could consider..."

Instead: "Based on my track record delivering X results, I'm targeting a salary range of $Y-Z." "I'm confident this aligns with market value for someone with my experience driving measurable outcomes."

4. Challenge Job Descriptions

If you're in a position to influence hiring language at your workplace, use tools like gender decoders (available free online) to analyze job descriptions before posting them. Push for language that emphasizes outcomes, impact, and results‚Äînot stereotyped traits.

The Bottom Line

Language isn't neutral. It's a weapon that's been used‚Äîconsciously or not‚Äîto justify paying women less for decades.

The words "supportive," "collaborative," and "nurturing" aren't bad. But when they're used to describe women's work while "driven," "ambitious," and "decisive" describe men's work, those words become a trap that keeps your salary suppressed.

You can't eliminate gendered language from the world overnight. But you can recognize it, refuse to internalize it, and strategically deploy the language that gets rewarded when you're talking about your own work.
